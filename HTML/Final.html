Data Exploration Summary¶

  • The primary factors that drive (and those that don’t drive) Yee-Haw transaction volume.

The factors in the dataset and their effect on transaction volume:

Snow - Snow data was provided as 0 in the dataset and thus is unknown about the relationship between snow and transaction volume.
Precipitation - There appears to be little to no association between precipitation and transaction volume.
Temperature - Temperature is cyclic with the seasons as expected. There appears to be little to no association between temperature and transaction volume.

Findings:

Holidays - There appears to be a strong association between transaction volume and days around major holidays.
Weekdays vs Weekends - There appears to be an association of a higher transacation volumne on weekends.

The key factors that drives transaction volume appears to be the day of the week (weekdays vs weekends) and days around major holidays. Precipitation, snow, and temperature do not appear to have a strong association with driving transaction volume.

  • The overall predictability of Yee-Haw transaction volume as a driver for inventory and resource planning.

The two factors that appear to drive transaction volume are day of the week and major holidays. The days with the highest transaction index are days around major holidays. (Valentines Day, Cesar Chavez, Easter, Mother's Day, Thanksgiving, Christmas, New Year's Eve). Also, weekends generally have higher transaction volume during the week than weekdays (except when holidays fall on a weekday).

In terms of predictability, based on the data, the highest transaction days tend to be around the major holidays and towards the end of the year in late November and December.

One thing to note is that major catastrophe events could also have an impact on transaction volume, such as the February 2014 winterstorm event , which appears to have affected the transaction volumes.

Model Methods and Selection¶

  • Methodologies for modeling daily Yee-Haw transaction volume in real-time going forward.

Machine learning algorithms were applied to the dataset in an attempt to generate a robust model for predictions. The following machine learning algorithms were tested on the dataset with 10-fold cross validation: linear regression, decision tree regressors, and random forest regressors.

The random forest model generated a similar results as linear regression, however, linear regression models are easier to interpret. The linear regression analysis shows that temperature and precipitation do not show a strong linear association with transaction index. Due to few factors in the dataset that showed a strong association with transaction volume, the linear algorithms tested do not appear to be strong estimators in modeling the daily Yee-Haw transaction volume. The model exhibited an error of 0.75 standard deviation when tested (RMSE of 0.053, mean and std of dataset is 0.533,0.073) and an R squared value of ~0.418 during fitting. This means that 41.8% of the variance can be explained from the model and parameters.

Forecasting can be based on general trends seen in the dataset but modeling daily transaction volumes appear to be difficult based on the current dataset and linear machine learning algorithms.

  • Methodologies for making regular algorithmic recommendations for inventory and resource planning.

The linear algorithms that were implemented are not robust enough to generate insightful findings. As the best model tested is linear regression which resulted in an R squared value of ~0.418 with RMSE of 0.053. Since the day of the week is the only parameter to show any statistical significance, this results in the linear regression model predicting based on day of week averages.

Current recommendations are based on using day of week averages for non-holidays and to expected the highest transaction days to occur around major holidays (such as Thanksgiving, Christmas) for inventory and resource planning.
